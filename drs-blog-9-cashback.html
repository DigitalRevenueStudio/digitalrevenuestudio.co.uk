Cashback — Running Quidco and TopCashback Simultaneously

Most guides tell you to pick one cashback platform. We are running both at the same time and always taking the best rate. Here is how it works, what we have earned so far and the friction points nobody else mentions.

Most cashback guides tell you to pick either Quidco or TopCashback and stick with it. The logic makes sense on the surface — simpler to manage, one account to track. But it means you are always leaving money on the table whenever your chosen platform has the lower rate.

Test 05 runs both simultaneously, with PayPal Rewards added as a third layer. Before every purchase, check both cashback platforms. Whichever offers the higher rate wins that transaction. Pay with the PayPal debit card where possible to earn points on top. Track everything. Report monthly. That is the experiment.

“Cashback only generates when there is something to buy. This is not a passive income stream — it is a discipline applied to spending you were going to do anyway.”

How the Experiment Works

The setup is straightforward. Both Quidco and TopCashback accounts are active from March 2026. Before any online purchase, both platforms are checked for the available cashback rate. The higher rate wins. The purchase goes through that platform's tracked link. Simple.

This is not a fixed-window experiment with a start and end date. Cashback is spend-dependent — if there is nothing to buy, there is nothing to report. So this runs as an ongoing monthly tracker. Every month, the results are published: what was purchased, which platform won, what rate was achieved, what was earned and what actually cleared.

Why both platforms? Cashback rates vary significantly between Quidco and TopCashback depending on the retailer and the time of year. The only way to consistently get the best rate is to check both. Running one platform exclusively means accepting a lower rate on every purchase where the other platform would have won.

The Friction Points Nobody Mentions

Most cashback guides make the process sound seamless. In practice there are friction points worth knowing about. We encountered one immediately on the first transaction.

Tracking failures happen

The NordVPN purchase did not track automatically through Quidco. This happens — ad blockers, browser settings or simply platform glitches can prevent the tracking pixel from firing. The result is that the cashback does not appear in your account and you have to raise a manual claim.

Manual claims require evidence

Quidco's manual claim process is straightforward but requires a screenshot of the transaction as evidence. This is reasonable and easy enough — but there was a further complication. The claim required a transaction ID from NordVPN. This is not displayed anywhere obvious in the NordVPN account dashboard. We had to contact NordVPN support directly to request it and are currently awaiting their response before the claim can be finalised.

Practical tip: When making any purchase through a cashback platform, immediately take a screenshot of the order confirmation page including the order or transaction ID. Do not rely on being able to find it later — some platforms make it difficult to locate. That screenshot is your evidence if tracking fails.

None of this is a reason not to use cashback. It is simply the honest picture of what the process actually involves when things do not track automatically. The claim is progressing and the £61.17 remains the expected outcome — it is just taking longer than an automatic track would have.

The Amazon Voucher

Alongside the cashback, Quidco offered a £20 Amazon voucher on the NordVPN purchase. The condition is that the subscription must be active for 31 days, after which the voucher is emailed within the following 30 days. This is in addition to the £61.17 cashback — not instead of it.

If both clear, the total return on an £83.23 NordVPN purchase is £81.17. That is effectively the full purchase price back. This is an unusually strong cashback return — NordVPN promotions are not always this generous — but it illustrates what is possible when you check rates before purchasing rather than after.

Bonus Earn — PayPal Rewards

Alongside the two main cashback platforms, there is a third layer worth mentioning. PayPal runs a rewards programme where spending on your PayPal debit card earns points. Reaching certain milestones unlocks cash rewards redeemable against future PayPal purchases.

By switching to the PayPal debit card for everyday purchases rather than a standard bank card, the first milestone of 1,000 points was reached in March. That unlocked a £10 reward redeemable on PayPal purchases.

Worth noting: PayPal rewards stack on top of cashback. If a purchase goes through Quidco or TopCashback and is paid for with the PayPal debit card, you earn both the cashback rate and the PayPal points simultaneously. This compounding effect is small on individual transactions but builds meaningfully over a month of normal spending.

What Comes Next

The next planned purchase through the experiment is Gatwick parking later this month. Both platforms will be checked, the better rate will be taken and the results documented. Monthly reports will cover every purchase, the platform that won, the rate achieved and what actually cleared into the account.

Cashback is not a significant income stream in the way that some of the other experiments are. It will never replace a salary. But applied consistently to spending you are making anyway, it generates a meaningful return over a year with no additional effort beyond checking two websites before purchasing.

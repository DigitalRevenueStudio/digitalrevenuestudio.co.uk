Troop Mastery:
Types, Tiers and F2P Strategy

Troops are involved in almost everything in Whiteout Survival. Whether you are rallying, defending, gathering, or competing in events, understanding the three troop types, the tier progression system and how troop choices interact with your heroes is fundamental to playing efficiently as F2P.

The Three Troop Types

Infantry, Lancers and Marksmen

There are three troop types in Whiteout Survival, each with distinct roles in combat. Understanding what each does and when to use them is the foundation of effective army building.

Infantry

The frontline tanks. Infantry have high Defence and Health, designed to absorb incoming damage and protect troops behind them. Their attack output is relatively low but they are essential in every march. In expedition battles, Infantry are targeted first due to their position.

Beats Lancers Loses to Marksmen Tank / Frontline

Unlocked at Infantry Camp (Furnace Level 7). T11 Helios Infantry requires War Academy research.

Lancers

The cavalry. Lancers have balanced attack and defence. From T7 onwards, Lancers gain a chance to strike Marksmen directly, bypassing Infantry positioning. They are fast, mobile and strong in rally compositions that need flexibility.

Beats Marksmen Loses to Infantry Flexible / Rally

Strong in SvS rallies and Alliance Championship compositions. Valued for their flexibility at higher tiers.

Marksmen

The ranged damage dealers. Marksmen have high Attack and Lethality — Lethality allows them to bypass enemy Defence and deal damage directly to Health. They are positioned behind Infantry in marches. Marksmen scale well into the late game and become increasingly powerful at higher tiers.

Beats Infantry Loses to Lancers DPS / Late Game

Strongest in Crazy Joe and ranged combat events. Their Lethality makes them increasingly effective as tiers increase.

The combat triangle matters most in PvP. In PvE events like Bear Hunt, the triangle is less relevant. In SvS, Foundry Battle and direct player attacks, stacking a troop type that counters your opponent's primary type can shift outcomes significantly. Ask alliance leadership what troop type they run before large engagements.

Troop Stats Explained

What Each Stat Actually Does

Every troop has seven stats. Most players only focus on the obvious ones — understanding all seven helps you make better upgrade decisions.

Stat
What it does
Power
Does not affect combat. Reflects the power added to your account when you train that troop. Higher tiers add more power.
Defence
Acts as armour. Absorbs incoming damage before Health is affected. Must be reduced to zero before Health damage can be dealt.
Attack
Basic damage output. Limited by the opponent's Defence — no Health damage until their Defence is depleted.
Health
The troop's HP. Once this reaches zero the troop is defeated and removed from battle permanently (unless in Hospital).
Lethality
Bypasses Defence entirely. Deals damage directly to Health. Particularly powerful against high-Defence opponents. Marksmen have the highest Lethality.
Speed
Uniform across all troops. Determines movement speed in battles. Different from Wilderness March Speed which affects map travel time.
Load
How much a troop can carry when gathering or looting. Relevant for gathering efficiency. Generally a low priority stat for combat-focused players.
Tier Progression

T1 to T11 What Changes Each Tier

There are 11 troop tiers in Whiteout Survival. Tiers 1 to 10 are unlocked by upgrading your troop training buildings (Infantry Camp, Lancer Camp, Marksman Camp). Tier 11 requires entering the Fire Crystal Age and researching in the War Academy. Each new tier provides substantially better stats — the power gap between tiers is much larger than the incremental gains from stat research within a tier.

Fire Crystal troops (FC versions): Once your state enters the Fire Crystal Age, troop training buildings can be upgraded past Level 30. This unlocks FC versions of your troops — the tier stays the same but stats improve significantly and special skills unlock at FC3 and FC8. FC troops are the late game power system.

The tier gap is enormous. A Furnace 26 player with T9 troops will overwhelm a Furnace 22 player with T7 troops regardless of hero quality or research stats. Always prioritise reaching the next tier over improving stats within your current tier.

Hero Synergies

Which Troops Match Your Heroes

Your hero lineup determines which troop type to focus your War Academy stat research on and which troop type to build more of. Matching troops to heroes compounds your combat effectiveness significantly.

Each hero in WOS belongs to one of the three troop types. To get the full benefit from a hero's skills, you need matching troops in your march. An Infantry hero leading a march with no Infantry troops will not trigger their troop-specific skills properly. Pairing heroes correctly is one of the most commonly missed fundamentals.

Infantry Heroes

Infantry is the dominant type in the early-to-mid game because the best accessible heroes align with it. Key Infantry heroes by generation:

Early game: Jeronimo (Gen 1, P2W but top-tier global buff), Sergey (Gen 1, F2P, strong defence and durability), Natalia (Gen 1), Flint (Gen 2, F2P).

Mid game: Logan (Gen 3, F2P), Hector (Gen 5, F2P, 25% troop attack buff — core F2P infantry lead), Wu Ming (Gen 6, P2W), Gatot (Gen 8, F2P — replaces Hector when available), Magnus (Gen 9, F2P — exceptional scaling with percentage-based buffs).

Late game: Eleonora (Gen 11, F2P — best infantry on release, viable into Gen 13), Elif (Gen 14, P2W), Hank (Gen 15, P2W).

With an Infantry-heavy lineup, prioritise Infantry Attack research in the War Academy. A march composition of roughly 50% Infantry is the standard starting point.

Lancer Heroes

Lancers provide flexibility and are essential in mixed rally compositions. From T7, Lancers gain a chance to target Marksmen directly. Key Lancer heroes by generation:

Early/Mid game: Molly (Gen 1, F2P), Philly (Gen 2, F2P — the primary healer, essential for all marches), Mia (Gen 3, F2P via Lucky Wheel — one of the longest-lasting heroes in the game, remains viable through Gen 15+), Reina (Gen 4, F2P via events), Renee (Gen 5, F2P — replaces Mia temporarily).

Late game: Fred (Gen 9, F2P), Freya (Gen 10, P2W), Karol (Gen 12, P2W), Dominic (Gen 14, P2W — takes over lancer crown in Gen 14), Estrella (Gen 15, P2W — percentage-based debuffs that scale into future generations).

Lancers are typically kept at ~20% of the army for flexibility. Players who have invested in Mia can justify a higher Lancer ratio given her exceptional longevity.

Marksmen Heroes

Marksmen have the highest Lethality in the game, bypassing enemy Defence entirely. They scale strongly into the late game and become the primary damage type at higher tiers. Key Marksmen heroes by generation:

Early/Mid game: Bahiti (Gen 1, F2P — early placeholder only, replace as soon as possible), Seo-yoon (Gen 2, F2P), Gwen (Gen 5, F2P — strong targeting in Exploration), Bradley (Gen 7, F2P via Lucky Wheel — 420% AoE damage, global 25% attack buff, one of the best F2P investments).

Late game: Blanchette (Gen 10, F2P via Lucky Wheel — best F2P damage dealer, competitive into Gen 13), Vulcanus (Gen 13, P2W — dominant AoE marksman that outclasses all older marksmen), Viveca (Gen 15, P2W).

Marksmen ratio typically sits at ~30% in general compositions and increases significantly in later game stages as Lethality scales. By Gen 8+ some players shift to 85%+ Marksmen for Bear Hunt specifically when their Marksmen Lethality stat significantly exceeds their Lancer Lethality.

Infantry is meta early, Marksmen scale late. The common F2P path: build Infantry-heavy in the early and mid game (Sergey, Hector, Gatot), maintain ~20% Lancers anchored by Mia and Philly, and gradually increase Marksmen ratio as Bradley then Blanchette come online. New hero generations arrive roughly every 80 days. Do not rebuild your army overnight — transition composition gradually as hero investments change.

Note on hero generations: This guide covers key heroes up to Gen 15. Generations continue beyond this as the game evolves. The general principle holds regardless of specific heroes: match troop type to hero type, prioritise the current generation's best accessible F2P hero in each class, and plan War Academy stat research to match your primary hero's troop type.

Promote vs Train

Upgrading Existing Troops vs Training New Ones

When a new tier unlocks, you have a choice: promote your existing lower-tier troops to the higher tier, or train fresh troops at the new tier from scratch. Both have their place and the right answer depends on context.

Promote when: you are not in an active event that scores on training completions. Promotion is generally more resource-efficient than training from scratch and gets you to a higher-tier army faster.

Train fresh when: an event is active that scores on troop training completions (Troop Training events, Power Sprint, Hero Rally). Fresh training completions score more points than promotions in most of these events.

Never fill queues with low-tier troops just to keep them running. An idle training queue is better than a growing pool of T4 troops that dilute your march composition. Quality over quantity at all times.

Decision Framework

Which Troops Are Right For Your Situation?

If you are a new F2P player (Day 0 to ~Day 100): Infantry first. The best early F2P heroes (Molly, Sergey, Alonso) all align with Infantry. Build ~50 to 60% Infantry, ~20% Lancers, ~20% Marksmen. Focus War Academy research on unlocking the next tier rather than stat improvements.

If you are a rally leader or R4/R5: Prioritise march capacity research heavily alongside tier unlocks. Your effective troop count per rally is one of your most important contributions. Composition varies by event — ask your alliance what they need most.

If you are focused on Crazy Joe: Infantry and Lancers for sending to allies. Keep Marksmen home for defence. Enable auto-join on all rallies. Marksmen defending your city are far more valuable than Marksmen in your outgoing marches during Crazy Joe.

If you are mid-to-late game with strong Marksmen heroes: Begin shifting composition toward higher Marksmen ratios (~30 to 40%). Their Lethality advantage scales strongly at T8 and above. Adjust War Academy stat research to match.

If you cannot figure out what composition to use: The general-purpose ~50/20/30 ratio (Infantry/Lancers/Marksmen) is a solid default that performs reasonably across most events and situations. It is the starting point used by a large portion of the F2P community.

Common Mistakes

What Weakens Your Army

Training low-tier troops to keep queues active. An army full of T5 and T6 troops alongside your T9s dilutes every march you send. The weakest troops in your march drag down average combat effectiveness. Idle queues are genuinely better than this.

Ignoring the combat triangle when choosing event compositions. Sending all Infantry into an event where your opponents are stacking Marksmen is a consistent disadvantage. It is not always possible to know your opponent's composition, but asking alliance leadership for guidance before major PvP events avoids obvious mismatches.

Over-committing to Infantry and never transitioning. Infantry is the correct primary type early. Players who never adjust their composition or War Academy research as better Marksmen heroes arrive leave meaningful late-game power on the table.

Sending Marksmen in Crazy Joe marches. Marksmen rarely contribute kills when sent to allies in Crazy Joe. Their highest value during that event is defending your own city. Keep all Marksmen home and send Infantry and Lancers to allies.

Letting the Hospital fill before combat. When your Hospital is full, defeated troops die permanently rather than becoming wounded. Always check Hospital capacity before any PvP engagement. This applies at every game stage.

Researching stat nodes before reaching your tier ceiling. This is covered in the War Academy guide but worth repeating here. Every stat improvement you research at T6 becomes irrelevant when you unlock T8. Tier unlocks first, stat improvements second.

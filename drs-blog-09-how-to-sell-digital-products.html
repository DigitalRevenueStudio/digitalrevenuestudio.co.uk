How to Sell Digital Products Online:
A Step-by-Step Guide for 2026

We have built and listed digital products across multiple brands from scratch. This is the process we actually followed — not a theoretical framework, but a practical guide based on what worked and what did not.

9 min read Digital Revenue Studio April 2026

Selling digital products is one of the most accessible ways to generate income online. No inventory. No shipping. No physical premises. You create something once and it can be sold repeatedly — to one person or ten thousand — without any additional work on your part.

That is the appeal. The reality is that most people who try it either choose the wrong product, set up on the wrong platform, or publish their product and wait for sales that never arrive because they have not thought about traffic at all.

At Digital Revenue Studio, we have been through the full process — choosing products, building a store, listing, pricing and driving traffic — across multiple active brands. This guide covers every step, in the order you should actually do them.

"You create something once and it can be sold repeatedly. But only if you build the audience first."

Step 1: Choose Your Product

The single biggest mistake people make is creating a product nobody asked for. Before you spend any time building something, you need a reason to believe people will pay for it.

Digital products fall into a few reliable categories. The ones that sell consistently tend to solve a specific, recurring problem for a defined audience:

Templates & ToolsSpreadsheets, documents, planners, trackers. High value because they save time on something the buyer does regularly.
Guides & EbooksWritten knowledge on a topic people search for. Works best when the author has direct experience, not just research.
Prompt LibrariesCurated AI prompts for specific tasks or industries. Strong demand as AI usage grows across all sectors.
Courses & WorkshopsStructured learning on a skill. Higher price point but more effort to produce and support.
Asset PacksDesign files, stock content, icons, fonts. Suits creatives with existing work to repurpose.
Software & ScriptsTools, calculators, automation scripts. Higher barrier to create but strong perceived value.

At Digital Revenue Studio, we started with what we already knew. Our construction project management background led to the NEC Contract Manager — a browser-based tool that solves a real, daily problem for a specific professional audience. Our content studio experience led to an AI prompt library and tools directory. Both products came from genuine domain knowledge, not trend-chasing.

The test before you build: Search for your product idea on Google. Are people asking questions about this topic? Are there existing products selling in this space? Competition is a sign of demand — not a reason to avoid it.

Step 2: Create It Properly

Digital products have no physical presence to reassure a buyer. The only signal of quality they have before purchasing is how the product looks and how it is described. A poorly presented product will not sell regardless of how good the content is.

A few principles we follow across all our products:

  • Format matters. A PDF guide should be properly designed, not a plain Word document export. A spreadsheet tool should be clean, labelled and intuitive. First impressions are made in the preview images, not the content itself.
  • Scope it tightly. A focused product that does one thing well outperforms a bloated product that tries to cover everything. Buyers want a clear outcome, not a comprehensive resource.
  • Name it for the outcome. "NEC Contract Manager" tells the buyer exactly what they get. "Construction Tools Bundle" tells them nothing useful. The name should answer the question "what does this do for me?"
  • Version and date it. Especially relevant for guides and tool directories. Buyers are more confident purchasing something that is actively maintained.

Step 4: Set Up Your Store

Once you have chosen your platform, setting up the storefront correctly matters more than most people think. Your store page is often the first impression a buyer gets — not the product listing itself.

When setting up on Payhip, we focused on three things:

  • Store branding. Upload a profile image, write a clear bio that explains who you are and what you sell, and make sure the store name matches how you present yourself elsewhere. Consistency builds trust.
  • Product presentation. Every product gets a proper cover image — something that looks professional at thumbnail size. The description is written for a buyer who knows nothing about you, covering what they get, what format it is in and why they should buy it from you specifically.
  • Pricing with intent. We do not underprice to compete. A £9.99 guide signals more value than a £1.99 guide on the same topic. Price based on the outcome you deliver, not on what feels safe.

Step 5: Price It Correctly

Pricing digital products is less science and more confidence. Most first-time sellers underprice badly. The cost of production is largely irrelevant to the buyer — what matters is the value of the outcome.

Our general framework across our products:

  • Under £10: Impulse purchase territory. Low friction, low deliberation. Works well for templates, small guides, prompt packs. Volume is the strategy here.
  • £10–£30: Considered purchase. Needs a clear outcome and credible presentation. Most of our mid-tier products sit here.
  • £30+: Requires trust. Works for professional tools, comprehensive guides and anything with a demonstrable ROI. Our NEC tools sit in this range because the audience — project managers — can immediately see the time saving.

One practical test: if you feel slightly uncomfortable with the price, it is probably about right. The instinct to drop the price is almost always fear rather than logic.

Step 6: Drive Traffic

This is where most digital product businesses fail. The platform does not bring buyers to you. Payhip handles the transaction — finding the customer is entirely your responsibility.

The traffic channels we use across our brands:

  • SEO and content. Every blog post on this site is written to rank for a search term our audience is already using. A well-ranked post drives consistent, free traffic indefinitely. This guide is an example of that approach in action.
  • Reddit. For the right niches — gaming, personal finance, professional communities — Reddit is an extraordinarily powerful traffic source. One genuine, helpful post to the right subreddit can send thousands of visitors. We document this approach as part of our WOS (Whiteout Survival Guide) brand build.
  • Pinterest. Underrated for content-based businesses. Pins index in Google search and drive long-tail traffic well beyond the initial post date. We run active boards across all our brands.
  • Email list. The only channel you own outright. We use Sender to build subscriber lists for each brand separately. Even a small, engaged list converts better than large cold traffic volumes.
  • Social proof. Testimonials, honest numbers and documented results — all the things we publish here at Digital Revenue Studio — build the kind of trust that turns a visitor into a buyer.

The uncomfortable truth about traffic: Most people want to skip this step. They list the product, share it once on social media and wonder why nothing sells. Traffic is not a one-time activity — it is an ongoing commitment that takes months to compound into reliable results.

Step 7: Build for Repeat Business

The hardest sale is the first one. Once someone has bought from you and received genuine value, the relationship changes entirely. They are far more likely to buy again, to recommend you and to become part of the audience that sustains the business long term.

A few things we do to build repeat business:

  • Email capture at checkout. Payhip captures the buyer's email as part of the purchase flow. Every buyer is a potential subscriber. We follow up with useful content, not just promotional emails.
  • Product bundles. We offer bundle pricing across related products — the NEC Template Bundle, NEC Manager Bundle and NEC Complete Bundle exist because buyers who want one tool often want the full set. Bundles increase average order value significantly.
  • Consistent publishing. Every new guide, blog post and tool we release brings previous buyers back. The audience you build is the asset — the products are almost secondary.
  • Honest documentation. At Digital Revenue Studio, we publish our real numbers. That transparency is not just a brand choice — it builds a level of trust that generic promotional content never can.
The Honest Summary

Selling digital products works. It is not passive income from day one — it is work, audience building and patience. The businesses that succeed are the ones that treat the product as the end of the process, not the beginning. Build the audience first. The product is easier to sell to people who already trust you.

The tools exist to make this straightforward. You do not need a development budget or a team. You need a product worth buying, a platform that handles the transaction, and a consistent strategy for getting the right people in front of it.

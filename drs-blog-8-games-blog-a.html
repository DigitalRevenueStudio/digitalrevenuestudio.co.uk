Can You Actually Earn Money Playing Games? We Tested the Flow First.

Before starting the experiment clock, we proved every platform end-to-end. Here is what we found — the real fees, the cooldown timers and why one platform did not make the cut.

Before any DRS experiment starts, the platform has to be proven. That means signing up, completing offers, reaching the minimum cashout threshold and actually receiving the money. No platform enters the experiment on faith — it has to have demonstrated it pays out.

Test 04 is earn by playing games. The premise is simple: gaming apps and reward platforms pay you to download games, complete offers and reach certain milestones. The question is whether the earnings are meaningful, the process is straightforward and the platforms are worth your time.

We tested four platforms before the experiment clock started. Here is everything we found.

“The point of the flow test was not to maximise earnings. It was to understand exactly how each platform works before we go in properly.”

Why We Test the Flow First

Most “earn by playing games” content jumps straight to earnings claims. We do not. The flow test exists for a specific reason: to understand the mechanics of each platform before the experiment begins, without contaminating the experiment itself.

During the flow test, we deliberately avoided the most lucrative offers — the ones that would be available and unrestricted during the experiment weeks. The goal was to prove the payout process, understand the fees and identify any friction points. The numbers from this phase are intentionally conservative.

What the flow test covered: Sign-up process, offer availability, minimum cashout threshold, withdrawal method, fees, time to receive payment and any account requirements. Each platform had to pay out successfully before being included in the experiment.

The cleanest platform of the four. Built up the £5 minimum over three days using surveys and easy cash options including paid emails. No in-game purchases were required and no fees were applied to the withdrawal.

Cashout was directly into PayPal. One important note: the PayPal email address must match the email you registered with on InboxPounds. The payment was seamless and arrived without issues.

Flow verdict: Clean, straightforward, no surprises. Strong start to the experiment.

EarnLabs processes payments in dollars despite being used from the UK. The minimum cashout is $5 and withdrawals are handled via Tremendous, their reward partner, who takes a small processing fee — in our case $0.25. The PayPal cashout was seamless and arrived within 30 minutes.

One thing to be aware of: Tremendous emails a claim link to your registered email address. You need to click this to release the payment. Missing that email means missing your payment — worth checking your spam folder.

Flow verdict: Slightly more steps than InboxPounds but straightforward once you know the process. The dollar-to-pound conversion slightly reduces the effective return.

Freecash In — Week 3
5 daysTo reach cashout
~1hr/dayTime invested
$5.00Minimum cashout
£2.43Received (flow test)

Freecash was the most complex platform to flow test. A few initial offers did not track correctly, requiring a 72-hour wait for resolution. To reach the minimum withdrawal we also needed to make a £20 in-game purchase — a cost we factored in deliberately and accepted to prove the flow. That spend is reflected in the numbers.

Freecash also operates a cooldown system. Earnings from completed offers are not immediately withdrawable — they are held for a set period before release, which can be up to 14 days depending on the offer. At the time of writing, £19.98 remains in cooldown pending release. This is a significant detail that most coverage of Freecash does not mention.

Withdrawal options include PayPal, Amazon vouchers, Google Play, Apple, Visa Prepaid and crypto. PayPal is confirmed available to UK users. Fees are 5% plus a $1 PayPal consumer fee. Like EarnLabs, transactions are processed in dollars despite UK usage.

Why include Freecash despite the lower flow test return? The platform has a significantly larger offer pool than the alternatives. Several tracked offers from the flow test are still pending resolution via a support ticket. The structural opportunity is stronger than the flow test number suggests — the experiment week, with no restrictions on offer selection, should tell a very different story.

Prograd Not in experiment
3 daysTo reach cashout

£0.33Net after all costs

Prograd technically paid out. The problem is structural. The platform charges a £2 flat processing fee on every withdrawal, meaning a £5 cashout becomes £3 before you start. Their reward partner Tremendous then takes an additional 0.68p for PayPal processing.

The offer selection is limited compared to the other platforms. To reach the £5 threshold during the flow test, the only viable route involved a £1.99 in-game purchase — not because we chose it, but because the available offers were insufficient to reach the minimum without it.

We considered whether the fee structure would improve with higher cashout amounts. It does — Prograd reduces the fee percentage as the withdrawal amount increases. But the limited offer pool means building a significantly higher balance would take disproportionate time.

Why drop Prograd but keep Freecash? Freecash's low flow test return (£0.41 net after the £20 in-game purchase) is partly explained by untracked offers currently under review and a deliberately cautious offer selection. The platform has a large offer pool and the cooldown period is a timing issue, not a structural one. Prograd's problems — the flat £2 fee and limited offers — are built into how the platform operates. They do not improve with a better week. That distinction matters.
